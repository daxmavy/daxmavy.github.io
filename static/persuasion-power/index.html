Power & cost for a three-arm persuasion experiment¶

Against / neutral / for summaries. The design and the statistics are anchored to Röttger et al.'s ai-distortion persuasion follow-up (Study 5): we replicate their exact mixed model, take its fitted variance components, and run the power analysis through the same model by simulation.

Their model (from analysis/5_persuasion_study/persuasion_regressions_full_analyses.R):

$$\text{stance\_shift} \sim \text{condition} + (1\mid\text{reader}) + (1\mid\text{proposition})$$

a Gaussian linear mixed model (glmmTMB) on the oriented stance shift direction × (attitude_post − attitude_pre), 0–100 scale. Re-running this notebook shallow-clones their repo and refits the model.

2. Exact replication of Paul's model¶

We refit his RQ1 (paragraph_type: control/human/ai) and RQ2 (5-level condition) models with glmmTMB and check the average marginal effects against the numbers committed in his repo. We then read off the random-effect variance components — the inputs the power simulation needs. (Requires R + glmmTMB; the script is replicate_paul.R in this folder.)

def loo_corr(Xmat):
    Xmat = Xmat.reshape(len(yv), -1); pred = np.zeros(len(yv))
    for tr, te in LeaveOneOut().split(Xmat):
        sc = StandardScaler().fit(Xmat[tr])
        mdl = RidgeCV(alphas=np.logspace(-2,3,20)).fit(sc.transform(Xmat[tr]), yv[tr])

5. Power & cost — using Paul's exact model¶

Estimand: biased arm vs neutral arm (Δ = the for-vs-neutral mean shift; we sweep Δ ∈ {1,2,5,10} because the neutral arm won't behave like Paul's irrelevant control). We simulate the three-arm design under his model — stance_shift ~ arm + (1|reader) + (1|proposition) with the fitted variance components above and a 12-proposition pool — and read off the SE of the for-vs-neutral coefficient. It scales as $SE(N)=SE_{\text{unit}}/\sqrt{N_\text{readers}}$ (verified), giving $n = \big((z_{1-\alpha/2}+z_{\text{power}})\,SE_\text{unit}/\Delta\big)^2$.

From sim_power.R (glmmTMB, averaged over reps, stable across N): Design A (6 questions, 2/arm) $SE_\text{unit}=15.85$; Design B (3 questions, 1/arm) $SE_\text{unit}=22.76$. A direct check (validate_power.R): at the N predicted to give 90% power for Δ=3 (Design A, N=293), 200 simulated experiments reject in 92% of cases — the analytic formula is sound. Cost model is Paul's: £12/rater-hour, 1.5 min/paragraph + 2.5 min overhead.

At a realistic effect of Δ ≈ 3–4 (§1), £400 is roughly the floor for the biased-vs-neutral contrast on Design A (MDE ≈ 3.9), and £800 gives headroom (MDE ≈ 2.8). Both budgets detect the pro-vs-anti gap (≈ 2Δ) comfortably.

7. Recommendations¶

  • Design A (6 questions, 2 per arm) beats the 3-question design by ~20% at every effect size, with no fatigue penalty (§3).
  • Make the primary test pro-vs-anti (gap ≈ 2Δ) rather than biased-vs-neutral — ~4× cheaper than the table above.
  • Select propositions by lowest writer stance-extremity (§4), softest ~12–15 — enough to keep Design A free of within-person proposition repeats and to generalise.
  • Plan around Δ ≈ 3–4 (Paul's stanced paragraphs moved beliefs ~3.4–4.5 pts; summaries may move them less, so treat Δ ≥ 5 as optimistic).

Caveats. Single contrast at α = 0.05 (testing both pro and anti vs neutral with Bonferroni adds ~20% to N); SE_unit is for a 12-proposition pool with balanced within-reader arm assignment, and Paul's model assumes no treatment-by-reader/proposition heterogeneity (random intercepts only, exactly as preregistered).
