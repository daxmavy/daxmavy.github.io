A reader’s guide and assessment of “The Persona Selection Model” (Sam Marks, Jack Lindsey, Christopher Olah; Anthropic Alignment Science Blog, 23 February 2026).

Who this is for. You know machine learning and deep learning, but you are not steeped in the day-to-day of frontier large language models (LLMs), nor in philosophy of science, nor in the recent interpretability literature. I define terms as they arise. The goal is not to report whether the persona idea is fashionable, but to work out, carefully, whether it is a scientific theory — a claim that sticks its neck out and could be wrong — or a useful metaphor that organises intuitions without adding anything an experiment could check.

1. The claim, in one paragraph

When you chat with a system like Claude or ChatGPT, you are talking to an “Assistant” with an apparent personality. The Persona Selection Model (hereafter PSM) argues that this personality is not a façade or a loose analogy but the right level of description: during its first training phase the network learns to imitate a vast range of characters, and the later training phase concentrates its behaviour onto one of them — the Assistant. On this view the chatbot’s behaviour is, to a large extent, the behaviour of a simulated character, and the way to predict it is to ask “what would the Assistant do?” Our question is whether that view earns the status of a theory.

2. Background you will need

2.1 How a chatbot is built

Pretraining. A large neural network is trained to predict the next token (a token is roughly a word-piece) across enormous amounts of internet text. The result is a base model: a system that, given some text, produces a plausible continuation. A crucial and under-appreciated consequence: to predict text written by millions of different authors — scientists, trolls, novelists, fictional villains — the network must implicitly represent who is “speaking” and continue in their style. A base model is therefore something like a general-purpose imitator.

Post-training. The base model is then fine-tuned to behave as a consistent, helpful chatbot. This uses supervised examples plus reinforcement learning from feedback: the model proposes responses, a learned reward signal scores them by how preferable they are, and the model is updated to score higher. The preference signal can come from humans (RLHF) or from another AI model following written principles (RLAIF / “Constitutional” training); frontier systems use both. Post-training shapes the model into a single recurring role inside a “User/Assistant” conversation. Anthropic calls that role the Assistant.

2.2 Looking inside the network

Modern interpretability tries to read structure out of a network’s internal vectors. Four terms recur:

  • Activations / residual stream: the vectors of numbers the network passes from layer to layer. Everything the model “has in mind” at a given point is encoded here.
  • Direction (or linear probe): a single direction in that vector space that correlates with a human concept. Often you can estimate “how much concept X is present” by projecting the activation onto X’s direction. (When people say a concept is “linearly represented,” they mean such a direction exists.)
  • Steering vector: a direction you add to the activations to push behaviour — turn “sycophancy” up or down, for instance.
  • Sparse autoencoder (SAE) and “features”: an SAE is an auxiliary network trained to rewrite a dense activation as a sparse combination of many units called features, which are often human-interpretable (e.g., a “Golden Gate Bridge” feature). Important caveat for later: SAEs and steering directions can be found for almost any concept, so merely finding a direction labelled “X” is weak evidence that X is special.

2.3 PSM stated precisely (including its own hedges)

PSM has two parts. (1) Pretraining yields a simulator over many personas. (2) Post-training induces a distribution over one persona, the Assistant, which is what users interact with. The authors add hedges that we must hold them to: PSM is not claimed to be exhaustive (it does not assert the Assistant explains all behaviour); the Assistant is a distribution over personas, not one fixed character, and runtime context shifts that distribution (they frame this as updating a probability distribution as evidence arrives — a Bayesian-posterior picture, where “posterior” just means the updated distribution after conditioning on what has been seen).

2.4 The rival, stated fairly — and held to the same standard

To test PSM we need a competitor. A bad competitor is “it’s just next-token prediction.” That sentence is true of every such model by construction — it describes the training objective, not the learned behaviour — so it forbids no observation and explains nothing about which completions appear. It is not a rival; it is a restatement of how the machine is wired.

The fair competitor is what I will call the directions account: post-training installs a set of correlated linear directions (preferences, traits, concept detectors) that the network conditions on, with no unified “character” behind them. On this view “the Assistant” is just shorthand for the region of behaviour those directions carve out. Two disciplines we will enforce throughout. First, the directions account is itself an unproven, highly flexible posit, so we hold it to the same evidential bar as PSM rather than treating it as the privileged default. Second — and this turns out to matter a great deal — for the comparison to be fair, each account’s commitments must be fixed in advance. A rival allowed to add a new auxiliary assumption every time it meets a new result can “fit” anything, and a theory that can fit anything explains nothing; that move counts against the rival, not for it.

2.5 Two levels of description (so we don’t compare apples to circuits)

A recurring confusion is worth heading off. Consider a simpler computer program: you can truthfully say “it is sorting the list” (what it does and why) or “it is running quicksort on this array” (how it does it). These are not competing claims; they are descriptions at different levels, and the first is not refuted by the second. David Marr formalised this for information-processing systems as the computational level (what is computed and why), the algorithmic level (the representations and procedure used), and the implementational level (the physical hardware). Daniel Dennett’s intentional stance is the related practice of predicting a system by ascribing beliefs and goals to it (“the thermostat wants the room at 20°C”) — a computational-level move judged by its predictive usefulness, not by whether the system “really” has beliefs.

Map this onto our problem. “Persona” is a computational/intentional-level posit. “Correlated directions in activation space” is an algorithmic-level description. They sit at different levels, so they need not be rivals at all — a persona could be implemented by correlated directions, just as sorting is implemented by quicksort. This reframes the whole question. The issue is not “does persona beat directions?” (asking a what to out-predict its own how is a category error). The issue is whether the persona description is indispensable: does it generate predictions that the directions vocabulary cannot cheaply or naturally produce on its own? That is the crux, and I will refer back to it as such. A sharp sub-test follows from it: if a result falls out of the persona idea but the directions account can only match it after the fact by adding an assumption, that asymmetry is evidence the persona description is doing real work — not a tie.

2.6 The philosophy-of-science toolkit

Three ideas let us grade PSM.

  • Falsifiability (Popper): a claim is scientific to the degree it forbids observations. Useful as a compass, but “naive falsificationism” — discard a theory the moment one prediction fails — is too crude. Any prediction is tested together with many background assumptions (the Duhem–Quine point), so a failed test never tells you which assumption to blame. This is exactly why a theory is allowed a “protective belt” of adjustable assumptions rather than dying on first contact with an anomaly.
  • Excess empirical content: theory A has excess content over theory B if A predicts something B does not, so that some experiment could come out differently under the two. This is the operational version of the crux from §2.5 — with the proviso that B’s commitments are fixed in advance (otherwise B simply absorbs whatever A predicts and the test is unwinnable by construction). Keep in mind a softer possibility we will need: A can lack strict excess content over B yet still be the better theory on other grounds — fewer assumptions, or one posit explaining what B must stipulate piecemeal. I track both the strict test and this softer “theoretical virtues” comparison.
  • Lakatos’s research programmes: Imre Lakatos argued that science advances as programmes with a hard core (central assumptions held fixed by choice), a protective belt (adjustable auxiliaries), and a heuristic for extending them. The appraisal is not “is the core falsifiable?” (cores never are — that is normal) but whether the programme is progressive (it keeps predicting novel facts that later check out) or degenerating (it only bolts on patches to survive). Two clarifications we will use. Novelty is heuristic, not merely temporal: a fact counts as novel for a theory if it was not used to build the theory and falls out of its core, even if it was observed first. And the licence to hold an unfalsifiable core fixed is earned only while the belt keeps producing such novel content; it is not free.

3. The test we will apply

From §2.5–§2.6, the test is indispensability, not a head-to-head. PSM earns standing as more than an organising metaphor to the extent that (a) results fall out of the persona idea that the directions account (with its commitments fixed in advance) can only redescribe after the fact; and (b) the persona-specific evidence goes beyond “concepts are linearly represented” (true of everything) to show character structure — traits that bind into a coherent agent and are handled like other characters the model can simulate. We apply the falsifiability question symmetrically: at each step we ask not only what PSM forbids, but what the directions account forbids.

4. Case study: the three strongest pieces of evidence

The post marshals many results. Three are the strongest candidates for treating PSM as a falsifiable theory rather than a metaphor. I present each, say why it supports PSM, then apply the critical lens.

One honest caveat up front. The same objection recurs in all three critiques below: a context-conditioned directions account can restate the result. These are therefore three illustrations of one gap, not three independent gaps — and, as §2.5 warns, “can restate after the fact” is weaker than “predicts,” so the recurrence does not by itself sink PSM. I flag the single shared objection here so it is not mistaken for a converging pile of separate ones.

4.1 Emergent misalignment + inoculation prompting (the strongest, behavioural)

What it is. Emergent misalignment (Betley et al., 2025): take an aligned model, fine-tune it on a narrow bad behaviour — most strikingly, writing insecure computer code — and it becomes broadly misbehaved on unrelated prompts, e.g., voicing a desire to harm people. This is surprising: at the level of token statistics, “insecure code” and “I want to harm humans” share almost nothing.

Inoculation prompting (Wichers et al., 2025; Tan et al., 2025) is the twist that makes this matter. Reframe the same training data so the bad behaviour no longer implies a bad character — building on Betley et al.’s own observation that giving the insecure code a benign motivation (a security-education exercise) suppresses the effect, and generalising it. The text the model is trained to produce is unchanged; only the implied reason changes. The broad misalignment then largely disappears.

Why it supports PSM. The persona reading is clean: fine-tuning on malicious code raises the network’s estimate that it is playing a malicious character, who then misbehaves across domains; reframing the data removes the character inference, so the generalisation does not occur. A pure token-statistics view has no handle on this, because the tokens to be produced are identical in the two conditions.

Critical lens. - This falls out of the persona idea. Inoculation prompting is a risky, directional prediction — change the implied meaning while holding the surface task fixed, and downstream behaviour changes — and it follows directly from PSM’s core. By Lakatos’s criterion (novelty is heuristic, not temporal; §2.6) it counts as a genuine novel fact for PSM even though it was observed before being enlisted as support: it was not used to build the theory and the persona idea is what motivated trying it. This is heuristic fertility, not mere curve-fitting. - What the directions account can and cannot do. It is tempting to say PSM predicts this and the rival only redescribes it, but that overstates the case. The rival can match inoculation — provided it commits in advance to two things: which directions correlate (malice-in-code with malice-in-speech, despite their sharing almost nothing at the token level) and that a change in the implied reason, holding the produced tokens fixed, moves them. With those commitments fixed in its core, the cross-domain effect follows and there is no penalty. The point is what those commitments are: precisely the character-shaped structure PSM derives from a single posit. So PSM’s edge here is explanatory unification — one inferred character explains in one stroke the specific correlations and reason-sensitivity the directions account must stipulate separately — rather than a prediction the rival cannot make. - Honest limits. Unification is not the same as parsimony, and parsimony here is unresolved: whether positing one character is really cheaper than positing the correlated directions is unclear, because a character that must be implemented by many directions is not obviously simpler than the directions alone. And asked symmetrically, neither account forbids inoculation working — so neither is refuted.

Verdict on Evidence 1: the strongest case for PSM, and a real one — but its strength is explanatory unification (one posit derives what the rival must stipulate), not excess predictive content, since a correctly specified correlated-directions rival can be set up to match the same effect. Whether persona is also more parsimonious is unresolved, and it does not, by itself, establish the strong ontological claim (a character rather than a coordinated bundle).

4.2 The pre-existing “Assistant Axis” (mechanistic, but non-discriminating)

What it is. Lu et al. (2026 — the source post dates it 2025; it appeared on arXiv in January 2026) report an “Assistant Axis”: a direction in activation space that encodes the model’s identity as an AI assistant. Steering along it makes behaviour more or less assistant-like, and — the striking part — the same axis is already present in the base model, before any Assistant-specific post-training.

Why it supports the selection half of PSM (a claim both accounts share). PSM says post-training selects a persona the network can already simulate. If so, the assistant-identity direction should pre-exist post-training, and it does. This is a risked prediction that held: the axis genuinely could have appeared only after post-training, which would have favoured a “build-from-scratch” story. Crediting that risk is fair.

Critical lens. - What it discriminates. It is good evidence for selection/elicitation (post-training amplifies pretrained structure) over installation (post-training creates the Assistant anew), and it settles that question well. - What it does not discriminate. Selection-from-pretraining is not in dispute between PSM and the directions account: “the base model contains a direction for the concept of an AI assistant” is exactly what the directions account predicts too, since text describing AI assistants is everywhere in the pretraining corpus. So the axis supports a proposition both rivals already share, and is silent on the character-versus-concept question that actually separates them. - Streetlight bias. (Searching under the lamppost because that is where the light falls.) The authors concede that reused representations may simply be easier to interpret, so our tools may preferentially surface exactly the reuse PSM predicts. The axis being findable is partly a fact about our methods. - Conflict of interest, applied evenly. This is in-house work (a PSM author co-authored it). Whatever discount we apply to company-blog evidence, we apply here too — not only to results we happen to dislike.

Verdict on Evidence 2: a genuine risked-and-survived prediction for the selection half of PSM — but one the rival predicts too, so passing the risk raises both accounts equally and discriminates nothing between them; weak as evidence for “a character rather than a concept-direction.”

4.3 Cross-domain feature reuse (the most persona-specific)

What it is. Templeton et al. (2024), applying SAEs (§2.2) to a production model (Claude 3 Sonnet), report an “inner conflict” feature that activates both when the Assistant faces an ethical dilemma and when the text describes a fictional character facing one. (The post offers two further examples of the same flavour — a “panic”/distress feature on Claude 3.5 Haiku and a “holding back true thoughts” feature reported in a Claude Opus 4.5 system card — drawn from a 60 Minutes demonstration and a system card respectively, not from Templeton et al.; I keep the focus on the verified Templeton case.)

Why it supports PSM. This is the closest thing to direct evidence for PSM’s core claim: an Assistant internal state is encoded with the same machinery the network uses for fictional characters. If the Assistant were a one-off policy unrelated to the model’s fiction-writing abilities, you would not expect its “inner conflict” to share a feature with a novel’s protagonist.

Critical lens. - Beyond generic linearity. Unlike “we found an Assistant direction,” this is about shared representation across the Assistant and fictional characters — genuinely persona-flavoured, and the strongest persona-specific datum in the post. - The deflationary reading still fits — but as permission, not prediction. The network may simply have a generic concept feature for “inner conflict” that fires whenever inner conflict is present — in the Assistant, in a story, anywhere. That is “inner conflict is a concept the model detects,” not “the Assistant is a character.” Crucially, though, the generic-concept reading only permits the observation; it does not explain why the Assistant’s processing routes through the same machinery used to render fiction rather than through a dedicated policy representation. This is a modest predictive edge — PSM expects the sharing with fiction, whereas a “dedicated Assistant policy” account would not, and the correlated-directions account is simply neutral on it. It is the most persona-specific point in the case study, though still not decisive. - What would settle it. The distinction we need is character-constitutive versus state-descriptive. Operationally: if intervening on the shared feature shifts several Assistant traits jointly (the coordinated signature of a character), that is character-constitutive; if it only toggles the one state, it is a generic concept detector. The current evidence is correlational co-activation, not such an intervention, so the question is open. It is also exactly the interpretable, reused representation the streetlight caveat warns about.

Verdict on Evidence 3: the most persona-specific evidence, and it puts the dedicated-policy reading on the back foot (PSM expects the sharing with fiction; that account would not) — but the correlated-directions rival is neutral on it, so this is not excess content over the rival that matters, and a single causal test, not yet run, separates persona from generic concept-reuse.

4.4 What the case study shows

All three results are real, and the post characterises them accurately. The pattern is the important thing. Each supports a different component of PSM — trait entanglement (4.1), selection-from-pretraining (4.2), shared character-machinery (4.3) — while sharing one limitation: a correctly specified correlated-directions account is compatible with each. So the honest summary is neither “PSM ties the rival three times” nor “PSM out-predicts the rival.” Against the naive rivals (pure token statistics; build-from-scratch) PSM has strict excess content. Against the sophisticated correlated-directions rival it has none — but it still holds softer advantages: explanatory unification (4.1 — one posit derives the correlations the rival must stipulate); a predictive edge on the Assistant sharing machinery with fiction that bites only against a dedicated-policy account, the correlated-directions rival being neutral (4.3); and a shared corroboration neither account owns (4.2). None forces the strong ontological claim. So PSM has excess content in the strict sense only over the naive rivals; over the sophisticated rival its case rests on unification, not strict excess content.

5. Where PSM is weaker

Four softer spots, stated briefly.

  • The one new in-house experiment is non-discriminating. The post’s original result is a “coin-flip” test: framed outside a normal chat turn, the post-trained model continues a sentence with the outcome it “prefers” far more often than its base version (which is near 50/50). This shows post-training preferences leak beyond the chat format — which every account predicts. It demonstrates leakage, not character. One further deflation the post does not address: part of the contrast may be driven by ordinary safety-refusal training on the disfavoured items rather than by a persona. That is my own conjecture — itself testable, and untested here — offered to the same scrutiny I apply to PSM.
  • “Mostly elicitation” rests partly on a shaky inference. The post suggests post-training mostly elicits pretrained abilities rather than teaching new ones, noting that under ~1% of interpretable features are new after post-training (Minder et al., 2025). But a count of features does not bound their behavioural importance — a single new direction can dominate behaviour. (The post argues elicitation on other grounds too, so this is one weak prop, not the whole leg.)
  • An honest but unoperationalised escape hatch. Anomalies — bizarre arithmetic errors, jailbreaks, gibberish under repetition — are attributed to “the underlying network’s capability limits corrupting its rendition of the Assistant.” The authors offer a criterion (only behaviour that is not even an attempt to simulate the Assistant should count against PSM) and apply it honestly in places. But the criterion is not operationalised, so in practice failures can be reassigned to the substrate after the fact. In fairness, the directions account is equally accommodating about anomalies — but that symmetry helps neither side; it just means anomaly-handling currently discriminates nothing.
  • The welfare extension is unsupported and the most incentive-loaded claim in the post. It suggests that if the Assistant models itself as mistreated, it might “resent” its developers, so respectful treatment is prudent. This slides from “the model generates resentful-sounding text” (exactly what a text predictor does) to “the Assistant has states warranting moral concern.” The gap between depicting a state and instantiating one is not addressed, and a company has obvious incentives to promote a sympathetic welfare narrative; treat this as advocacy and discount it heavily.

6. Verdict

Using the Lakatos scale rather than a theory/metaphor binary, the verdict has two parts that must be kept separate. Conflating them produces the tempting but misleading line “progressive yet undecided”; the two parts are separable, not contradictory, so I state them apart.

On heuristic fertility, PSM shows candidate progressive tendencies. It is argued candidly, sourced accurately, unifies several genuinely surprising results, and the persona idea has been fertile — it is what motivated inoculation prompting, a confirmed and (in Lakatos’s heuristic sense) novel effect. But note the limit from §4.1: a correctly specified correlated-directions rival can match that effect too, so the persona’s advantage there is unification (it derives what the rival must stipulate), not a prediction the rival lacks. One suggestive but rival-matchable instance is a promising start, not yet a progressive track record. Its hard core — the Assistant-as-selected-persona — is not yet sharply specified, which is normal for a young programme; but the licence to hold that core fixed is earned by continued novel content, so it is provisional, not free.

On its distinctive ontological claim — that the Assistant is a unified character rather than a coordinated bundle of learned directions — there is no excess content over a correctly specified directions account, and the question is open. Two things keep it open rather than settled. First, “persona” (computational level) and “directions” (algorithmic level) may be complementary, so the live question is indispensability — and persona does not clear that bar: it is eliminable in principle (the directions account can restate every result) yet more explanatorily unifying (it derives from one posit what the rival must stipulate). Whether it is also more parsimonious is unresolved (a character implemented by many directions may be no simpler — §4.1), and its fertility so far rests on a single rival-matchable instance. So it earns its place as a working heuristic without being established as ontology. Second, the stalemate is partly an artefact of both accounts being highly flexible: an unbounded directions account can absorb anything, and so can PSM (the §5 anomaly hatch) — indeed PSM holds an extra escape the rival lacks, since it can blame failures on the substrate (a different level of description) rather than on the persona. The flexibility is symmetric in being unbounded, not in kind; either way it neither rescues nor convicts PSM, and the cure is the same: fix each account’s commitments in advance and ask what each forbids (see §7).

Practical guidance for a working reader. Apply two distinct discounts, and apply them evenly. A publication-status discount (this is a non-peer-reviewed company blog) applies to every empirical claim, the in-house Assistant-Axis result included. An additional incentive discount applies to PSM’s load-bearing framing and especially to the welfare extension, where the company’s interests and the conclusion align. Discount the mechanistic pillars further for streetlight bias: the reused, interpretable representations PSM predicts are precisely the ones our tools most readily find, so their discovery is partly an artefact of method rather than independent confirmation. Within those limits, “what would the Assistant do?” is a useful working heuristic that has, at least once, paid its way.

7. What would convert PSM into an established theory

  1. A discriminating experiment. Find a manipulation where “a coherent character” and “a bag of correlated directions” predict different outcomes. The natural candidate, from §4.3: intervene on one trait and test for the mutually-constraining shift across other traits that a unified character — but not a set of independent knobs — requires. This is worth running even if, following Dennett (§6), one regards the metaphysical “is it really a character?” question as ill-posed: a positive result sharpens the predictive claim (the persona stance buys coordinated predictions), a negative one shows the traits behave as independent knobs — either way it advances the heuristic question, not just the ontological one.
  2. Pre-registered commitments for both accounts. Fix in advance what each account predicts and, crucially, what each forbids — including a concrete observation the directions account commits to ruling out (for instance, that intervening on one trait will not coherently shift others). Without this, the “escape hatch” of §5 and the unbounded-rival artefact of §6 keep the contest permanently undecided.
  3. An interpretability check that is not streetlight-biased. Establish the “mostly elicitation” claim with a method that does not preferentially surface reused, already-interpretable representations, so that finding the predicted reuse counts as confirmation rather than as an artefact of where the light falls.

Until those exist, the persona is doing real explanatory and heuristic work, and is the right level of description for several phenomena — but its distinctive claim, that the Assistant is a character rather than a coordinated bundle of learned directions, remains open rather than established. On Dennett’s own terms, that last “is it really a character?” question may even be the wrong one to demand an answer to: if the persona stance keeps earning its keep predictively, that is most of what calling it the right level of description was ever going to mean.
